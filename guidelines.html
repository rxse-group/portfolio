Academic Journey

Stepping into tertiary life felt like entering a different kind of space — not louder, not busier, just heavier with responsibility. It’s the first time I’ve had to balance who I am and who I’m trying to become in such a structured environment. I’m still adjusting to that weight.

Studying while managing my own creative and digital work has been more demanding than I expected. It’s not just about finding time — it’s about shaping it properly. I’ve learned that if I don’t create a rhythm for myself, the days start pulling me in opposite directions. That’s where discipline quietly becomes a survival skill.

This journey has been less about grades and more about self-organisation. I’m learning how to prioritise, how to work with intention, and how to stay present when everything moves fast. It’s made me sharper. More aware of what I want and how I operate.

I’ve also become more honest with myself. Some days I’m overwhelmed. Some days I’m proud. Most days, I’m somewhere in between — figuring things out in real time. And I’m realising that this mix is normal. It’s part of the process of becoming someone who can stand firmly in both the academic world and the creative world without losing themselves.

I’m still growing into the balance. But I’m getting there.

Group Work — (Tuckman's stages)

Working on a group assignment mostly by myself wasn’t the situation I expected, but it ended up teaching me more about teamwork than any functioning group could have.

I found myself moving through Tuckman’s stages almost as if I had a team — except it was just me handling every layer. Forming became about identifying the roles that should exist. Storming turned into sorting out the confusion that comes from taking on everything at once. Norming arrived when I finally shaped a clear structure for the work. And performing was the moment the assignment started flowing because I had organised it well enough to move without friction.

It made me think a lot about what good collaboration actually requires. It isn’t the number of people — it’s alignment, shared rhythm, and clarity. I had to create that alone, and it showed me that I naturally work best when there’s order, visuals, and defined responsibilities. I function better with a system.

Even without a full team, the project pushed me to apply the model in a real way. It helped me understand my own working style — how I plan, how I take initiative, and how I navigate situations where the structure is missing.

Next time I work with a proper group, I’ll step into it with a clearer sense of how to guide everything toward that performing stage.
Not as a theory — but something I’ve actually had to live through.
